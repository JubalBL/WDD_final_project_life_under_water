Solutions

Environmental conservation

Marine protected areas

Seas, oceans, and lakes with special preservation measures, where human activity is restricted or limited to ensure their conservation and protect their resources. An example is the Ross Sea Region, a deep bay covering 2,060,058 km² south of New Zealand, home to one third of the world’s Adélie penguin population.

Coral Restoration

It aims to recover reefs that have suffered damage or alterations to their physical, biological, or functional integrity due to changes in water temperature, ocean acidification, shifts in climate patterns, and physical impacts from ships or storms. Coral fragmentation, artificial structures used as substrates for new corals, and the selection of “super corals” are among the most commonly employed techniques for their restoration.

Sustainable fishing

Selective fishing, not trawling


With the aim of capturing only target species, employing a more sustainable exploitation, and minimizing bycatch, selective fishing is promoted instead of trawling, which negatively impacts fish populations and destroys seabed habitats, altering ecosystems in the long term.

Seafood product traceability


It provides information about the origin and management of seafood, offering transparency between consumers and companies, ensuring that marine products come from responsible and sustainable catches. An example is GDST, an international initiative created in 2017 that seeks to develop standards from capture or farming to the point of sale.

Solutions to Marine Pollution

Wastewater Sanitation


Wastewater is treated through physical, chemical, and biological processes so it can be safely returned to the environment—either discharged into the sea or reused. There are multiple methods of wastewater treatment, and the choice depends on the level of waste, debris, or contaminants present in the water.

Marine seabed cleanup programs


According to Greenpeace, the ocean receives around 200 kilos of waste every second, and many of these materials can remain on the seabed for hundreds of years. These cleanups must be carried out in a balanced and well‑planned way. To reduce this impact, trained divers perform dives to remove solid waste and help preserve the ecosystem.
